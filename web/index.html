PVPI on SFAIX

Immutable audit logs.

Three participants, a coordinator A and two collaborators B and C, train together. Every action is written down and signed, the records of each round are bundled and sealed, and the seal is posted to a public ledger. The result: a complete, tamper-evident record of who did what, and when, without anyone being able to quietly change it.

How one round works

The base problem, kept deliberately simple. Three fixed parties want a shared result, but none will share private data: A holds x, B holds a secret y, C holds a secret z. Each round, B and C reveal only a computed value, never the secret, and the averaged result feeds the next round. Press Play to watch one round, or step through it.

Step 1 / 6
1

A shares a number

The coordinator A sends the current model value x to both collaborators.

2

B & C compute privately

Each adds its own secret value: B makes x + y, C makes x + z. The secrets never leave.

3

They sign & send back

Each signs its result and returns it to A. The signature proves it really came from them.

4

A combines

A averages the results into the next model value, and signs that too.

5

A stores records off-chain

A bundles the four signed records and pushes them to the off-chain store (IPFS-style), getting back a content id (CID) that addresses the bundle.

6

A anchors on-chain

A posts a tiny anchor to the public ledger: the batch fingerprint plus that CID, signed and linked to the previous anchor.

● A | coordinator● B, C | collaborators ● off-chain | the bundle store● on-chain | the public ledger
01

How are logs created?

Every action becomes one signed record. Think of it as filling in a form, taking a fingerprint of it, and pressing a personal wax seal on top.

1. Write it down

The facts of the action: who, what, which round, the value, the time.

2. Take a fingerprint

A short, unique fingerprint of that exact content. Change one letter and the fingerprint changes completely.

3. Sign it

The author signs the fingerprint with their private key, so only they can produce that signature.

4. A sealed record

The record + its signature. Anyone can check the seal; nobody can forge it.

A round produces four records: A's broadcast, B's and C's signed results, and A's combined result. Together they say exactly who did what, and the signatures make it undeniable.

02

How are they stored?

Three layers, from the full detail up to a tiny public commitment.

The log

Every signed record kept in full, tagged with who created it and which round it belongs to.

The batch

All of a round's records bundled into one package with a single fingerprint, held in the off-chain store and addressed by a content id (CID). Edit anything inside and both the fingerprint and the CID change.

The seal

A tiny commitment posted to the public ledger: the batch fingerprint plus the off-chain CID and a link to the previous seal. Append-only.

Order matters: the bundle is stored off-chain first, which produces its CID; only then is the on-chain seal posted, carrying that CID. The bulky records stay off-chain and just the small seal goes on the ledger, so the public cost stays tiny and roughly constant per round.

04

Why can't they be faked or changed?

Nothing is locked in a vault. Instead, any change is instantly detectable. The signed, sealed log is the feature: it proves who did what, and lets anyone verify it.

  • What it proves who sent what, in which round, and whether anything changed.
  • Who it serves partners first, external auditors later.
  • Why it matters collaboration between non-trusting parties becomes defensible.

The four safeguards that make it detectable

The signature

Only the author's private key could have signed a record. Tamper with it and the signature stops matching.

The batch fingerprint

Every record folds into one fingerprint for the round. Change, drop or reorder one and it no longer matches.

The address

The off-chain bundle is addressed by its own fingerprint. Edit the file and its address changes, breaking the reference.

The chain

Each seal points back to the previous one. Removing or altering a block breaks the link for everything after it.

See it for yourself: in the live demo, edit a record and watch the signature, the batch fingerprint and the chain all turn red.

05

What does it cost?

Computing: tiny, just an ordinary laptop, no special hardware. Each round needs only a handful of signatures and a few fingerprints; all of it finishes in milliseconds.

Storage: the detailed records grow with the work and live in the off-chain store (cheap). The public ledger only ever holds a tiny seal per round, and its size stays about the same whether a round has four records or four hundred.

That separation is the whole point: keep the heavy data cheap and off to the side, anchor only a small, constant-size proof on the public ledger.
